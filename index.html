How I think

— 01

Understand before you push back

My default when entering a new team or a difficult relationship is to listen first. I'm trying to understand the technical reality, the history, and what's driving someone's position before forming a view. Most resistance has a reason behind it. Finding that reason is usually more useful than trying to overcome it.

— 02

The platform is a commercial asset, not infrastructure

Data platforms get treated as back-end plumbing. I treat them as product. Every architectural decision has a cost, a trade-off, and a downstream commercial consequence. When making roadmap decisions I'm always asking: what does this unlock for customers, and what does it cost us to maintain?

— 03

Start minimal, let usage tell you what to build next

I have a strong bias toward shipping something smaller sooner over designing something comprehensive later. Not because I don't value rigour — but because the best requirements come from real usage, not planning sessions. The semantic data layer and BYOD both started small. In both cases the right expansion became obvious once people were actually using it.

— 04

Pull in the right people, then decide

I'm not the smartest person in every room and I don't try to be. What I am good at is knowing which room I need to be in, and who needs to be in it with me. When facing a decision I don't have full context on — technical, commercial, or organisational — I'd rather take an extra day to get the right people around the problem than move fast in the wrong direction.

Commercial Product Mastercard · SessionM
Productising BYOD — From Shadow Process to Governed Platform Capability
How I took an informal, ungoverned data workaround and turned it into a compliant, self-serve product capability adopted by 20+ enterprise customers.
The Problem

Enterprise customers on SessionM wanted to enrich their loyalty and campaign data with proprietary data from their own ecosystem — data from their other apps, clickstream behaviour, user-specific attributes. The demand was real and legitimate. The problem was how it was being met.

External data was already flowing into the platform through S3 — but informally, inconsistently, and with no governance whatsoever. Different customers were doing it differently. There was no audit trail, no compliance controls, no data contracts, and no standardised onboarding process. Legal and compliance had no visibility into what external data was entering the platform or how.

What I Decided

The instinct might have been to shut it down until something proper was built. That would have been the wrong call — customers were relying on it and sales was actively using the capability in conversations with prospects. The right move was to productise it: take what was already happening, understand it properly, and build it into something governed, scalable, and sellable.

The hardest design challenge was the tension between flexibility and standardisation. Customers had 23 different external data source types between them — each with their own formats, schemas, and ingestion requirements. Engineering needed standardisation to build something sustainable. Customers needed flexibility to bring in data that didn't conform to a single template.

How I Approached It

I designed an end-to-end data onboarding process in collaboration with integration engineers and data engineers — covering ingestion frequency, data classification, retention policies, audit logging, and access controls. Critically, I introduced a data contract for each customer onboarding external data: a formal agreement defining the structure, format, and quality standards their data needed to meet before entering the platform.

Compliance and legal requirements were built into the ingestion framework from the start rather than bolted on afterwards. The goal was a product that customers experienced as flexible and self-serve, but that engineering and compliance experienced as controlled and maintainable.
What It Unlocked

BYOD was adopted by 20+ enterprise customers across 23 external data source buckets. Customers could onboard their own data independently, without routing requests through engineering. Sales could present it as a formal, governed product capability rather than a bespoke arrangement. And for the first time, compliance had full visibility and control over what external data was entering the platform.

The work received recognition from both the Head of Engineering and Head of Customer Delivery — an acknowledgement that it had solved a real problem for the business, not just the technical teams.

What I Learned

The most valuable product work isn't always building something new. Sometimes it's looking at what's already happening informally — the workarounds, the shadow processes, the things people are doing because there's no proper solution — and turning that into something real. Those are often the highest-ROI opportunities on a roadmap because the demand is already proven. The only question is whether you can make it safe and scalable enough to own it properly.

Leadership Mastercard · SessionM
Rebuilding Trust Between Product and Engineering
How I inherited a broken product/engineering dynamic, won over a resistant Director of Data Engineering, and turned a dysfunctional relationship into a high-performing one.
The Problem

When I took over as the primary product manager for the data engineering team, the relationship between product and engineering was broken in both directions. Product was throwing requirements over the wall. Engineering was ignoring decisions they disagreed with and building what they wanted. And sitting at the centre of it was the Director of Data Engineering — who also led the team day-to-day — who had been burned by previous product managers and had largely stopped believing the function added value.

The honest picture is that product management had genuinely failed this team before I arrived. Requirements were poor, follow-through was inconsistent, and the engineering team had learned — reasonably — not to trust what product told them. The Director of Data Engineering was a genuinely difficult character, but his skepticism wasn't irrational given what he'd experienced.

When the most senior person on the engineering side is skeptical of product management, the whole team takes their cue from that. It wasn't just a personal dynamic. It was the operating culture.

What I Did

I didn't start with process changes or new frameworks. I started with conversations — 1-1s where I listened far more than I talked. I wanted to understand what had gone wrong before me, what he actually needed from a product manager, and where the technical complexity was that previous PMs had failed to appreciate.

The goal wasn't to win an argument. It was to understand his reality well enough that when I did push back — and I did push back — it came from a place of genuine understanding rather than ignorance dressed up as authority.

Over the following months I invested time in the technical architecture, brought him into roadmap decisions before conclusions were reached rather than after, and was consistent about following through on what I said I would do. No surprises. No requirements that landed without context.

The Turning Point

The shift was gradual, then suddenly obvious. The escalations stopped. Problems that would previously have been routed around me — taken directly to senior stakeholders to force a decision — started coming to me instead. He began solving things with me rather than around me. That's when I knew the relationship had genuinely changed.

The Impact

Once the dynamic between us shifted, the broader team followed. Engineering started contributing ideas in planning sessions rather than just estimating tasks handed to them. Product decisions were respected and implemented as intended. The atmosphere changed — collaboration replaced friction. And the escalations to senior stakeholders about product management stopped entirely.

What I Learned

When you inherit a broken relationship, the history isn't your fault but the repair is your responsibility. Investing time in understanding someone's technical concerns before pushing back, being consistent, delivering on what you say — these aren't extraordinary things. But in a context where the bar had been set so low, doing the basics well was enough to shift the dynamic entirely.

That takes longer than fixing a process. But it lasts longer too.

Platform Strategy Mastercard · SessionM
Building a Semantic Data Layer — Alignment, Cost Trade-offs & Stakeholder Pressure
How I navigated a fractured stakeholder dynamic, pushed back on a costly real-time demand using user research, and got a contentious decision approved — by getting structured when the room got loud.
The Problem

SessionM's data platform had no semantic layer. Teams queried raw and staging data directly, each interpreting the same tables differently. Conflicting numbers, eroding trust, and a constant stream of ad hoc data requests to engineering that should never have reached them. Analytics couldn't self-serve. The problem was visible to anyone paying attention across team conversations — but nobody had named it as a single thing to fix.

I identified it, made the case, and took ownership of defining and building it.

The Real Challenge — Alignment, Not Architecture

The technical work was manageable. The hard part was driving alignment between the people who needed to agree on what the semantic layer should actually do.

Our Director of Product and our Data Architect were not understanding each other. The Director had a habit of using imprecise language, changing requirements between sessions, and being the loudest voice in the room — which created significant pressure on the Architect and made it difficult to reach stable decisions. The most contentious issue was a demand for real-time data. As a requirement for a layer that needed to aggregate certain fields, real-time would have been technically complex and commercially irresponsible — the cost implications were significant.

Rather than let the loudest voice win, I worked closely with the Architect to develop several options for product leadership — each with clearly documented trade-offs, refresh frequencies, aggregation scope, and cost implications. The goal was to take the conversation out of the realm of opinion and into the realm of evidence.
The Decision

I also went back to users. The demand for real-time data was coming through loudly, but when I conducted user interviews I struggled to uncover concrete use cases that justified it. Customers were asking for real-time without fully understanding what it meant or what they'd do with it differently. That's a pattern worth taking seriously — stated preferences and actual needs are not always the same thing.

Armed with the options analysis and the user research findings, I pushed for a 1-hour refresh cycle with aggregations kept deliberately limited to only what was strictly needed. It was approved by product leadership.

Where It Landed

Delivery was still in progress when Capillary Technologies acquired SessionM. The MVP was defined, the approach was agreed, and the build was underway. I had planned to track adoption by analysing query counts against the semantic layer over time — a simple but reliable signal of whether teams were actually using it or reverting to raw data.

What I Learned

The most valuable thing I did on this project wasn't the product definition — it was acting as a translation layer between a Data Architect under pressure and a Director of Product creating that pressure. Getting technically precise people and strategically-minded people into genuine dialogue requires someone willing to hold the space, stay factual, and resist the pull of whoever is loudest. That's a product management job, and one of the more important ones.
